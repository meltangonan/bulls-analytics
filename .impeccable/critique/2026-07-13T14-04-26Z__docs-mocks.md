## Anti-Patterns Verdict

The system does not look like generic AI output. The collegiate masthead, Bulls palette, local voice, and analytical honesty give it a real point of view.

The main visual tell is a dashboard/report reflex: repeated rounded cards, pale panels, small gray labels, and dense tables. Summer League is clean but often feels assembled from modules rather than art-directed around a specific basketball finding. Season Shape escapes that pattern because its data mark is also its hero image.

The Impeccable detector returned `[]`, exit 0, on `house.py`. This is unsupported-Python behavior, not validation. Evidence came from all 16 committed PNGs at native, feed, and grid sizes; code/design-system inspection; and saved local references.

## Overall Impression

The architecture fork is not the visual bottleneck. `house.py` is a sound identity frame and correctly leaves composition free. The repository is much more precise about the recurring header/footer than about how the body of a post becomes beautiful.

The system needs a visual grammar above the house layer: one dominant object, named composition families, image roles, density modes, comparison grammar, chart-mark options, an emphasis budget, and feed-size exit checks. These should begin as design principles and post-local experiments, not as a new component library.

## What's Working

1. Season Shape is premium-adjacent. Its irregular red/black silhouette communicates scale, motion, and emotion before the axis is read. Annotations pace the story, and the endpoint face turns abstraction into a human payoff.
2. The current masthead is a strong thumbnail signature. Academic M54, a red emphasis unit, and the white frame provide recognition without needing a logo.
3. The existing shelf already contains multiple useful families: shape story, face-led comparison field, ranked board, heatmap, spatial court, and dense evidence table.
4. The YAGNI boundary is correct: post-specific art direction stays local until a second real use proves a component.

## House-Layer Verdict

- Keep the canvas, font pair, margins, footer pair, and export contract.
- Keep the white frame; allow bounded red, black, pale, image, or chart fields inside the body when the story earns them.
- Replace the verbal rule “exactly one red title word” with “one red emphasis unit, usually one word.” This preserves recognition without forcing awkward copy.
- Treat the kicker as optional, as the code already does.
- Do not add chart, card, hero, or layout archetypes to `house.py`.
- Prove title variations locally before adding universal fitting modes. Full-width fitted titles are a strong signature, but every future claim should not be forced into the same width and cadence.

## Priority Issues

### [P1] Claim hierarchy is weaker than format hierarchy

Summer League foregrounds “TEAM SNAPSHOT,” “SHOT PROFILE,” and the series masthead. The viewer is given categories of information before being told what happened. Caleb's 74.0% TS, Sellers' inefficient volume, Essengue's low-volume performance, and Swain's struggles should not use the same dominant composition.

**Direction:** Every feed-facing slide gets one declarative basketball claim. The chart, face, and supporting metrics become evidence for that claim. Series identity remains present but subordinate.

**Level:** Post-local first; later DESIGN principle.

### [P1] The system lacks named composition families

The shelf has useful forms, but the default middle-density report layout is better documented than the alternatives. Without an explicit vocabulary, future scripts are likely to reproduce cards and tables because they are easy and repeatable.

**Direction:** Define five starting archetypes: Shape Story, Player Hero, Comparison Field, Ranked Board, and Dense Evidence. The first four are feed-facing; Dense Evidence is normally a later carousel/detail slide.

**Level:** DESIGN-level guidance, proven through post-local mocks.

### [P1] Feed-size review is not an explicit design gate

At approximately 288x360, Season Shape retains its story. Summer cover tables, heat grids, lineup tables, court labels, footers, and gray rail labels often collapse into texture or disappear. Final 300-DPI export improves crispness but does not make text physically larger in the feed.

**Direction:** Require 360x450 feed and 180x225 grid checks. At 360, the claim, hero, and one supporting contrast must survive. At 180, the dominant silhouette and brand signature must remain recognizable.

**Level:** DESIGN and posting-workflow gate; possible footer adjustment only after visual proof.

### [P2] Rounded cards are becoming the default visual language

Four nested snapshot cards and six repeated profile cards are clean but generic. The soft gray UI vocabulary conflicts with the sharper collegiate masthead and flattens emotional range.

**Direction:** Use cards only for truly discrete modules. First test open typographic groupings, flat bands, sharper slabs, direct labels, rules, overlap, and one grouped field. Create depth through scale and layering, not shadows or more containers.

**Level:** DESIGN principle, executed post-locally.

### [P2] Player imagery has no complete art-direction grammar

Circular headshots work as labels; the red ring works as payoff. Gray silhouettes look unfinished, and tiny faces do not deliver the stopping power the system attributes to them.

**Direction:** Circle = functional identity label; red-ring circle = payoff; bust/cutout = hero. Define crop, quality, boundary-breaking, fallback, and licensing rules. If a usable hero image is unavailable, design the composition without a human focal rather than displaying a gray bust.

**Level:** DESIGN-level rule, then one post-local hero experiment.

### [P2] Red is branded but semantically overloaded

Red currently means Bulls, title accent, made shot, positive value, above baseline, and payoff. Repetition weakens its ability to tell the viewer what matters.

**Direction:** Declare red's role per composition. Default priority: focal Bulls subject first; positive/negative only when a visible baseline establishes that grammar. Allow one large solid-red payoff treatment in the body.

**Level:** DESIGN-level.

## Recommended Visual Grammar

1. Keep the frame recognizable; art-direct the body.
2. One post, one dominant visual object.
3. Make the insight the image whenever possible.
4. Use red and black as meaningful mass, not interface trim.
5. Use circles for identity and cutouts for heroes.
6. Create depth with scale, overlap, cropping, and bounded fields—not card stacks or shadows.
7. Prototype locally and promote only after the second real use.

## Starting Composition Families

| Family | Best for | Hero |
|---|---|---|
| Shape Story | Season arcs, runs, collapses, trends | Memorable data silhouette |
| Player Hero | One-player claim or breakout | Cutout/portrait plus one decisive mark |
| Comparison Field | Player vs player, before/after, availability/impact | Distance, delta, or opposing forms |
| Ranked Board | Top players or lineups | Faces plus one encoded ranking measure |
| Dense Evidence | Tables, heatmaps, complete box-score context | Supporting detail, usually later in a carousel |

## Component Verdicts

- **Premium:** current masthead, Season Shape silhouette, causal/fan annotations, crisp faces used as data labels, payoff ring.
- **Useful:** clean bars, stacked labels, pale bounded fields, direct tables when the story is genuinely rank-led.
- **Generic when repeated:** gray chips, soft rounded cards, zebra tables, faint court diagrams.
- **Needs proof:** hero cutouts, sharper score/stat bands, alternative annotations, small multiples, range/distribution marks, dark or saturated body fields.

## Persona Red Flags

**Distracted Bulls fan:** Season Shape communicates a rise and collapse instantly. Summer League often communicates only that statistics are present.

**Design-literate basketball-viz follower:** The flagship feels authored; the repeated cards and pale modules read as a competent Matplotlib report template.

**Low-vision feed viewer:** Solid red/black mass and the masthead perform well. Fine gray court lines, tables, labels, qualifiers, and footers are vulnerable.

## Questions to Consider
